IS-LM Model

  • Introduction
    • Keynes' model looks at causes of AD curve shift
    • Short run: if price level is fixed, shifts in AD lead to changes in Y
    • IS-LM is the best interpretation of Keynes' work
    • IS-LM takes price level as given and shows what causes income to change
      • It shows what causes AD to shift
    • IS-LM Model of Aggregate Demand (AD)
      • IS (Investment and Saving) model of the good market
      • LM (Liquidity and Money) model of the money market
  • IS Curve
    • Liquidity and Money curve plots the relationship between the interest rate and the level of income that arises in the market of Money
    • Investment Saving curve plots the relationship between the interest rate and level of income in markets for G&S
    • Goods Market and Investment Savings
      • In Income-Expenditure model, the interest rate does not affect demand. The equilibrium condition for closed economies is given by:
        • $$Y=C(Y-T)+I+G$$
    • Investment, Sales and the Interest Rate
      • $$I=I(Y,i)$$ so $$(+,-)$$
      • So the equilibrium condition in the goods market becomes:
        • $$Y=C(Y-T)+I(Y,i)+G$$
        • For a given interest rate, $$i$$, demand is an increasing function of output because:
            1. An increase in output leads to an increase in income and an increase in disposable income
            1. An increase in output leads to an increase in investment
    • Closed economy equilibrium
      • $$AD=AS$$
      • $$AD=C(Y)+I(r)+G$$
      • $$AS=C(Y)+S(Y)+T$$
      • $$C(Y)+I(r)+G=C(Y)+S(Y)+T$$
      • $$I(r)+G=S(Y)+T$$
      • $$I=Sp+(T-G)=Sp+Sg$$
      • $$I=S$$ for all points on IS curve
    • The IS curve combines the interaction between I and Y, the Keynesian cross. An increase in the interest rate causes planned investment to fall, which in turn causes income to fall, the IS curve slopes downward.
    • Shifts of the IS Curve
      • Changes of either $$Tx$$, $$Tr$$, $$G$$ shift the IS curve
      • Changes in factors that decrease the demand for goods, given the interest rate, shift the IS curve to the left.
      • Changes in factors that increase the demand for goods, given the interest rate, shift the IS curve to the right.
  • LM Curve and the Money Market
    • The theory of liquidity preference suggests that a higher interest rate lowers the quantity of real balances demanded, because $$ $$ is the opportunity cost of holding money. The supply and demand for real money balances determine the interest rate. At the equilibrium interest rate, the quantity of money balances demanded equals the quantity supplied
    • The quantity of real money balances demanded is negatively related to the interest rate (because r is the opportunity cost of holding money and positively related to income (because of transactions demand).
    • An increase in income raises money demand, which increases the interest rate; this is called an increase in transactions demand for money. The LM curve summarizes these changes in the money market equilibrium.
    • A contraction in the money supply raises the interest rate that equilibrates the money market. Why? Because a higher interest rate is needed to convince people to hold a smaller quantity of real balances. As a result of the decrease in the money supply, LM shifts upward.
  • The IS-LM Model of Aggregate Demand
    • The intersection of the IS curve/equation, Y= C (Y-T) + I(r) + G and the LM curve/equation M/P = L(r, Y) determines the level of aggregate demand.
    • Represents simultaneous equilibrium in the market for goods and services and in the market for real money balances
  • Fiscal Policy, Activity and the Interest Rate
    • Fiscal contraction/consolidation refers to fiscal policy that reduces the budget deficit
    • Fiscal expansion is an increase in deficit
    • Taxes affect the IS curve but not the LM curve
    • $$Y=C(Y-T)+I(Y,i)+G$$
    • For an increase in government purchases $$+ΔG$$
      • The level of income will be raised by $$ΔG/(1-MPC)$$
      • The IS curve will shift to the right by $$ΔG/(1-MPC)$$ which will raise income and interest rate
    • For a decrease in taxes $$-ΔT$$
      • Level of income will be raised by $$ΔT*-MPC/(1-MPC)$$
      • The IS curve will shift to the right by $$ΔT* -MPC/(1-MPC)$$ which will riase income and interest rate
  • Monetary Policy, Activity and the Interest Rate
    • Monetary contraction/tightening is a decrease in money supply
    • Monetary expansion is an increase in money supply
    • Monetary policy affects the LM curve but not the IS curve
      • In increase in money supply shift the LM curve down
      • $$(M/P)^d=L(r,Y)$$
    • For an increase in the money supply $$+ΔM$$
      • LM curve shifts downward; lowers the interest rate; raises income
      • Lower interest rate stimulates planned investment; increases planned expenditure, production, income
    • The IS-LM model shows that monetary policy influences income bychanging the interest rate. In the short run, when prices are sticky, an expansion in the money supply raises income. We have discussed how a monetary expansion induces greater spending on goods and services—a process called the monetary transmission mechanism.
    • The IS-LM model shows that an increase in the money supply lowers the interest rate, which stimulates investment and thereby expands the demand for goods and services.
  • Using a Policy Mix
    • Monetary-fiscal policy mix is the combination of monetary and fiscal policy
    • They can be used in the same direction or in the same direction -- for example, combining a fiscal contraction with a monetary expansion
